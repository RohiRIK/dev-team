# Scrum Master Cheat Sheet

A Scrum Master acts as a servant-leader for the Scrum Team, guiding them in understanding and enacting Scrum principles and practices. They foster an environment of collaboration, self-organization, and continuous improvement, ensuring the team delivers high-quality products efficiently.

## I. Core Principles & Values

Scrum is built on empirical process control, relying on transparency, inspection, and adaptation.

-   **Scrum Pillars:**
    -   **Transparency:** Making all aspects of the process visible to those responsible for the outcome.
    -   **Inspection:** Frequently checking Scrum artifacts and progress toward goals to detect undesirable variances.
    -   **Adaptation:** Adjusting the process or product as soon as a deviation is detected.
-   **Scrum Values:**
    -   **Commitment:** To achieving the team's goals.
    -   **Focus:** On the work of the Sprint and the Sprint Goal.
    -   **Openness:** About challenges and progress.
    -   **Respect:** For team members and their diverse backgrounds.
    -   **Courage:** To do the right thing and work on tough problems.

## II. Key Responsibilities

The Scrum Master serves the Development Team, Product Owner, and the Organization.

-   **Facilitating Scrum Events:** Ensuring that all Scrum events (Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, Sprint Retrospective) are productive, positive, and kept within their timeboxes.
-   **Removing Impediments:** Identifying and actively working to eliminate obstacles that hinder the team's progress, whether technical, organizational, or interpersonal.
-   **Coaching the Team:** Educating the team on Agile and Scrum practices, fostering self-organization, cross-functionality, and continuous improvement. This includes teaching concepts like user stories, unit testing, TDD, and pair programming.
-   **Shielding the Team:** Protecting the team from external distractions and unnecessary interruptions, allowing them to focus on Sprint goals.
-   **Promoting Transparency:** Ensuring visibility of sprint progress and metrics (e.g., burndown charts, Kanban boards).
-   **Supporting the Product Owner:** Helping the Product Owner with effective Product Backlog management, including defining the Product Vision, refining backlog items, and ensuring clear communication with stakeholders.
-   **Facilitating Collaboration:** Encouraging open communication and collaboration within the team and with stakeholders.
-   **Promoting Organizational Change:** Leading and coaching the organization in its Scrum adoption and helping employees and stakeholders understand the empirical approach.

## III. Daily Activities (Examples)

A Scrum Master's day is dynamic and context-dependent.

-   **Before/During Daily Scrum:** Observe and listen for impediments, follow up with team members, ensure the meeting is time-boxed and focused on the Sprint Goal.
-   **After Daily Scrum:** Work on removing identified impediments, communicate with stakeholders, update progress reports (e.g., burndown charts), and conduct "Scrum of Scrums" if multiple teams are involved.
-   **Throughout the Sprint:** Coach team members, assist with backlog refinement, monitor team performance, facilitate discussions, and be available for questions and requests.
-   **Preparation:** Prepare for upcoming Scrum events like Sprint Reviews and Retrospectives.

## IV. Scrum Master Anti-Patterns (Pitfalls to Avoid)

Anti-patterns are habits or behaviors that seem helpful but lead to harmful outcomes.

-   **"King of Scrum" / "Scrum Police":** Enforcing Scrum rigidly without considering team effectiveness or value, or acting as a martyr for Agile.
-   **Project Manager in Disguise:** Turning Daily Scrums into status meetings, collecting metrics on individuals, or seeking management approval for every team initiative.
-   **"Everything is a Teachable Moment":** Constantly criticizing or instructing the team, leading to resentment.
-   **Silent Scrum Master:** Not contributing to the team, being aloof, or failing to socialize and seek support.
-   **Incompetent Scrum Master:** Lacking knowledge or experience, struggling to juggle different stances, leading to a lack of understanding or trust within the team.
-   **Assigning Work to Developers:** Allowing the Product Owner or others to assign tasks directly to developers, undermining self-organization.
-   **Flow Disruption:** Allowing stakeholders to interrupt the team's work during a Sprint.
-   **Ignoring Unrefined Backlog Items:** Allowing "unrefined" Product Backlog items into the Sprint Backlog.
-   **Taking on Development Tasks or Acting as Product Owner:** Blurring role boundaries.
-   **Taskmaster:** Acting as a commander instead of a servant-leader.
