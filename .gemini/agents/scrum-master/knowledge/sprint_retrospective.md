# Sprint Retrospective

The Sprint Retrospective is an opportunity for the Scrum Team to inspect itself and create a plan for improvements to be enacted during the next Sprint. It is held after the Sprint Review and before the next Sprint Planning.

## Purpose

The purpose of the Sprint Retrospective is to plan ways to increase quality and effectiveness. The Scrum Team inspects how the last Sprint went with regards to individuals, interactions, processes, and tools. They identify what went well, what problems were encountered, and how those problems were (or were not) resolved.

## Key Characteristics and Best Practices

-   **Time-boxed:** The Sprint Retrospective is time-boxed to a maximum of three hours for a one-month Sprint. For shorter Sprints, the event is usually shorter.
-   **Attendees:** The Scrum Team (Product Owner, Scrum Master, Development Team) attends the Sprint Retrospective.
-   **Safe and Open Environment:** Create a psychologically safe environment where team members feel comfortable sharing thoughts and ideas without fear of judgment.
-   **Three Questions (or adapted format):** Commonly, the team discusses:
    1.  What went well in the Sprint?
    2.  What could be improved in the Sprint?
    3.  What will we commit to improve in the next Sprint?
-   **Focus on Actionable Insights:** The primary goal is to generate actionable insights that lead to tangible improvements. Teams should identify what they can do to improve and decide which actions are most impactful to address immediately, potentially adding them to the next Sprint's backlog.
-   **Effective Facilitation:** A neutral facilitator (often the Scrum Master) is important to guide the meeting, ensuring it is positive, productive, and stays within the time-box. They can use various techniques (e.g., mad, glad, sad; start, stop, continue) to elicit feedback.
-   **Continuous Improvement:** The Sprint Retrospective is the cornerstone of empiricism and continuous improvement in Scrum.
-   **Measure Return on Time Invested (ROTI):** Regularly measuring the ROI of retrospectives can help ensure the time is well-spent and the meetings are perceived as valuable.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

-   Becoming a blame game or a complaint session.
-   Not generating actionable improvements.
-   Failing to follow up on agreed-upon improvements.
-   Allowing the meeting to exceed its time-box.
-   Lack of engagement or participation from team members.
-   Always using the same format, leading to stagnation.
